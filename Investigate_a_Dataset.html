Introduction

Dataset Description

The no-show appointments dataset compiles information regarding medical appointments in Brazil, specifically focusing on patient attendance behavior. This dataset, comprising 110,527 records, aims to explore the factors influencing whether patients show up for their scheduled appointments. The data was sourced from Kaggle, providing a comprehensive view of various aspects related to medical appointments.

The dataset encompasses 14 features for each patient, including:

  • ScheduledDay: Tells us on what day the patient set up their appointment.
  • Neighborhood: Indicates the location of the hospital.
  • Scholarship: Indicates whether or not the patient is enrolled in the Brazilian welfare program Bolsa Famlia.
  • No-show: Indicates whether the patients showed up for their appointment or not (No means showed up while Yes means didn't show up).

The list of all features include:

Feature Dtype
0 PatientId float64
1 AppointmentID int64
2 Gender object
3 ScheduledDay object
4 AppointmentDay object
5 Age int64
6 Neighbourhood object
7 Scholarship int64
8 Hipertension int64
9 Diabetes int64
10 Alcoholism int64
11 Handcap int64
12 SMS_received int64
13 No-show object

Question(s) for Analysis

  1. What is the strength and direction of the correlation between age and the date difference between scheduling and attending appointments, as measured by the Spearman correlation coefficient?
  2. How does the distribution of patient age vary between attendance and absence at appointments?
  3. Is there a relationship between the waiting time and the likelihood of patients attending?
  4. Does sending SMS reminders to patients have a significant impact on their attendance for appointments?

def plot_age_vs_attendance(df ,colmname):
    """
    Plot a histogram of age distribution comparing attendance and absence.

    Parameters:
    - df (DataFrame): DataFrame containing the data.
    """
    # Create two separate DataFrames for attendance and absence
    df_attend = df[df["attended"] == 1][colmname]
    df_absent = df[df["attended"] == 0][colmname]

    # Plot histograms using pandas plot function
    ax = pd.DataFrame({'Attend': df_attend, 'Absent': df_absent}).plot.hist(bins=10, alpha=0.5)
    # Calculate the mean and standard deviation of the data

def plot_age_vs_attendance(df, colname):
    """
    Plot a box plot of age distribution comparing attendance and absence.

    Parameters:
    - df (DataFrame): DataFrame containing the data.
    - colname (str): Name of the column for comparison (e.g., 'Age').
    """
    # Create two separate DataFrames for attendance and absence
    df_attend = df[df["attended"] == 1][colname]
    df_absent = df[df["attended"] == 0][colname]

    # Calculate the mean and standard deviation of the data for attendance and absence
    mean_value_attend = np.mean(df_attend)
    std_value_attend = np.std(df_attend)
    mean_value_absent = np.mean(df_absent)
    std_value_absent = np.std(df_absent)

    # Create a box plot
    plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
    df_boxplot = pd.DataFrame({'Attend': df_attend, 'Absent': df_absent})
    boxplot = df_boxplot.boxplot(showfliers=True, return_type='dict')

    # Annotate mean and standard deviation for attendance
    plt.text(1, mean_value_attend, f'Mean: {mean_value_attend:.2f}', color='r', fontsize=10, ha='right')
    plt.text(1, mean_value_attend - std_value_attend, f'SD: {std_value_attend:.2f}', color='g', fontsize=10, ha='right')
    plt.text(1, mean_value_attend + std_value_attend, f'SD: {std_value_attend:.2f}', color='g', fontsize=10, ha='right')

    # Annotate mean and standard deviation for absence
    plt.text(2, mean_value_absent, f'Mean: {mean_value_absent:.2f}', color='r', fontsize=10, ha='right')
    plt.text(2, mean_value_absent - std_value_absent, f'SD: {std_value_absent:.2f}', color='g', fontsize=10, ha='right')
    plt.text(2, mean_value_absent + std_value_absent, f'SD: {std_value_absent:.2f}', color='g', fontsize=10, ha='right')

    # Annotate outliers and sample size
    for i, (outlier, sample_size) in enumerate(zip(boxplot['fliers'], [len(df_attend), len(df_absent)])):
        for j, value in enumerate(outlier.get_ydata()):
            plt.annotate(f'Outlier: {value:.2f}\nSample Size: {sample_size}', xy=(i + 1, value), xytext=(-10, -30),
                         textcoords='offset points', ha='right', fontsize=8, color='b')

    # Set legend labels and line styles
    legend_labels = [f'Mean: {mean_value_attend:.2f}', f'SD: {std_value_attend:.2f}',

    legend_lines = [plt.Line2D([0], [0], color='r', linestyle='--'),  # Mean line
                    plt.Line2D([0], [0], color='g', linestyle='--'),  # SD lines
                    plt.Line2D([0], [0], color='r', linestyle='-'),  # Mean line
                    plt.Line2D([0], [0], color='g', linestyle='-')]  # SD lines

    # Show legend with custom labels and line styles
    plt.legend(legend_lines, legend_labels, loc='best')

    # Set labels and title
    plt.xlabel("Attendance Status")
    plt.ylabel(colname)
    plt.title(f"Box Plot of {colname} by Attendance Status")

    # Show plot
    plt.show()

Interpretation:

  • A high chi-square statistic (usually above 3.84 for a significance level of 0.05) indicates a rejection of the null hypothesis, which states that there's no association between the variables. In this case, the value is very high, suggesting we can reject the null hypothesis.
  • A p-value of 0.0 signifies a very low probability (less than 0.05) of observing this chi-square statistic by random chance. This strengthens the evidence that a real connection exists between receiving an SMS and attendance.

Based on the chi-square test, there is a statistically significant relationship between receiving an SMS and patient attendance. This means that patients who receive SMS reminders are more likely to attend their appointments compared to those who don't receive them.

  5. Causation vs. Correlation: While correlations were observed between certain variables and attendance, the analysis does not imply causation. Additional research is needed to understand the underlying factors influencing attendance.

Limitation:

Class Imbalance:

The class imbalance between patients who attended (88207) and those who didn't (22314) can be a limitation in Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) and statistical testing in a few ways:

  1. Misleading Visualizations:

    • Distribution Skew: Histograms, box plots, and other visualizations that rely on distribution can be skewed towards the majority class ("attended"). This can mask patterns or trends present in the minority class ("not attended").

  2. Statistical Test Inapplicability:

    • Assumption Violations: Many statistical tests assume a normal distribution of data for both classes. With a significant imbalance, normality might not hold for the minority class, rendering the tests unreliable.

  3. Limited Insights:

    • Focus on Majority: EDA techniques might inadvertently focus on understanding the "attended" group due to its overwhelming presence. This can lead to overlooking important information about the factors influencing non-attendance.

Data Types:

Nine columns (gender, neighbourhood, scholarship, etc.) are of type category. This suggests the data might be stored as strings or codes representing categorical variables. While this is common for categorical data, it can limit some statistical analysis compared to numerical representations.

Finally, Moving forward, further research could delve into the specific factors contributing to attendance patterns, such as socioeconomic factors, health conditions, or geographical location. Additionally, incorporating qualitative data or patient feedback could provide deeper insights into attendance behavior and help inform strategies to improve appointment attendance rates.
